Origin Field Theory (OFT) is a speculative theoretical framework that investigates whether law selection can be modeled as a stability-weighted conditioning process over a space of possible physical and informational configurations.

The original OFT proposal framed existence as arising from an atemporal relation between an Origin Field, understood as a measure space of possible configurations, and a Focus relation that conditions this space into stable, law-governed structures. In that formulation, time is not treated as fundamental; instead, temporal order appears as an emergent index of informational stabilization. The theory also introduced observer capacity as one term in a broader stability functional, alongside self-consistency, algorithmic simplicity, and fragility.

The updated version narrows the claim substantially. Rather than presenting OFT as a confirmed theory of consciousness, cosmology, or physical law selection, the current work treats it as a research program for constructing, testing, and auditing stability-weighted priors over finite model ensembles.

In this updated form, OFT asks a more limited and testable question:

Can a declared stability functional be used to construct a reproducible prior over a finite ensemble of possible laws, rule systems, or bounded candidate universes?

The v1.5.x implementation explored this question using elementary cellular automata as a toy universe ensemble. The complete 256-rule ECA space was used as a finite, fully enumerable testbed. Each rule was evaluated using explicit proxy measures for stability-related properties, and those scores were converted into a Gibbs-style prior distribution. The result was not a claim that cellular automata are physical laws, but a controlled demonstration of how a stability-based law-selection framework can be made explicit, reproducible, and auditable.

Version 1.6.4 extends the framework beyond the ECA toy ensemble by adding a separate physical-extension layer. This extension defines a finite bounded candidate space over two physically motivated parameters: the cosmological constant ratio Λ/Λobs and the gravitational constant ratio G/Gobs. Each candidate is mapped to a CMB shift-parameter approximation, allowing the stability prior to be compared against public Planck-derived observational constraints.

The physical extension compares the stability prior against three baselines: a uniform prior, a Jeffreys-proxy prior, and a constrained maximum-entropy log-space prior. It also includes resolution diagnostics through a 200×168 grid, prior-only heatmaps, prior-likelihood overlap analysis, sensitivity tables, figures, tests, manifest validation, SHA-256 checksums, and PDF documentation.

The updated framework emphasizes:

- finite declared model ensembles,
- explicit scoring assumptions,
- reproducible prior construction,
- observable-map extensions,
- grid-based likelihood calculation,
- Bayes-factor comparison against baseline priors,
- robustness and resolution diagnostics,
- null-model comparison,
- uncertainty and sensitivity checks,
- manifest validation,
- and clear separation between methodological results and physical claims.

The central supported claim is methodological: stability-weighted prior construction can be formalized, implemented, audited, and compared against baseline priors over finite candidate ensembles.

The current release does not claim to prove the full Origin Field ontology, establish consciousness as a fundamental physical operator, replace ΛCDM, modify quantum mechanics, or confirm a new mechanism of cosmological law selection. The physical extension is a diagnostic bridge, not a full cosmological analysis. Full physical validation would require domain-grade cosmological modeling, full likelihoods, nuisance-parameter treatment, and independent expert review.

In short, the updated Origin Field Theory should be understood as a bridge between speculative law-selection philosophy and reproducible model-selection methodology. It preserves the original motivation — explaining why stable law-like structures might be favored — while replacing unsupported certainty with finite-ensemble demonstrations, explicit assumptions, observable diagnostics, and falsifiable research pathways.
